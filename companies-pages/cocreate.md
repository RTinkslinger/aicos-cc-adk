## Marketscan & Breakdown by Trinika 2025-09-12 

Hi Team,

Adding the breakdown of market landscape and workflow in the excel here

Quick summary of key questions below:

![](cid:3199483326*image001.png@01DC22BC.55A4C510)

Is the problem big enough?

- Yes, because in a content/video production process, three prep tasks - proxy creation, renaming/restructuring, and audio–video sync - together take ~5-7% of the total production budget and ~10-15% of assistant editor time.

- Example: a 60-second ad or micro-drama with 4 cameras and separate sound typically needs 20-30hrs from AE before the first cut - iteration speed is higher for microdrama so faster speed req

- TAM (for these three steps): Global ~$3B; US ~$1.2B.

- SAM (automation wedge): Global ~$1.5B; US ~$0.5B.

- At 10% penetration of SAM: ~$150M ARR global; ~$50M ARR US

Big gaps in workflow today?

- Camera card to storage: For most cameras, copying from card to local drive and cloud is still manual. Only Canon paired with Frame.io (Adobe-owned) auto-copies to both.

- Transfer & naming: Cameras save files as C0001 or A002C012; editors need script-friendly names like Scene3_Take5. Renaming remains manual.

- Project & bins: Folders nomenclature varies from agencies to agencies – some do it by - day, by actor, or scene. No tool auto-restructures into clean bins/folder by scene/shot/take.

- Audio–video sync: Tools handle clear lip-sync, but struggle when lips aren’t visible or timing is creative. Example: a voice-over on a montage, or an off-screen car-crash sound.

Why incumbents haven’t built this?

This is a narrow, vertical play. Big players tend to buy vertical solutions - The category is highly acquisitive.

- Adobe Acquisitions

- Backlight Acquisitions

Company creation in the space? (Blackmagic example)

- Blackmagic Design (DaVinci Resolve), ~$600M revenue, bootstrapped.

- Proxy Generator can watch a folder and auto-make smaller proxies; a 10-minute file can be split into ~30-second proxy clips.

- Auto Sync can line up multiple cameras with a separate sound recorder.

- The gap: after proxies and basic sync, clips still need correct names and clean bins by scene/actor, and tricky timing like narration or off-screen sounds still needs human judgment. That’s the CoCreate wedge.

Cloud and Storage Issue

- Two Part - Problem

- Project to cloud

- Remaining gaps:

GTM (as per my understanding; haven’t seen the deck)

- Enter the Project/file/folder prep stage which incl. renaming/reorg, creating projects/tagging, and A/V sync

- Deliver as an in-tool extension, save 20–30 hours per project.

- Acquisitive space; natural buyers include Adobe, Blackmagic, Backlight.

Talked to a few creators/freelancers – here are the pushbacks I’m hearing:

- Will this be a completely separate software or integrated inside existing editing tools? People don’t wants to install another heavy editing app - even top-end Apple laptops slow down when running big editing programs like Adobe

- How will audio/video sync actually work? Lip-sync is easy, but the harder cases - dubbed content, internal dialogue, or off-screen sounds like a car crash (~30-40% market share). In these situations, audio and video lengths often don’t match (e.g., a 1-min video with 2-min audio). So, difficult for AI to handle - it still requires some creative judgment call.

AI is changing content creation, but post-shoot workflows will still exist

- AI can now generate both video and audio together (e.g., avatars speaking in cloned voices). This works well for short, simple formats.

- But in real production, audio is often added separately - for dubbing, narration, sound design, or multiple language versions.

- Video generation is more useful today because it replaces expensive shoots (cameras, sets, actors). Audio, on the other hand, is cheaper to produce - which is why teams prefer to add it later for flexibility and realism.

- This keeps the room open for post-shoot automation - proxy creation, renaming, restructuring, audio–video sync-up, and related prep tasks that remain bottlenecks even in an AI-first workflow.

## SME Calls by Trinika 2025-09-13 

Hi Team,

Had a chat with Zohrab Reys (runs agency 1g4T; has worked on ads with Cultfit, Acko, etc.) and Dheeman Garg (freelance photographer; also shoots Z47 podcasts) to get a ground-level understanding of pain points in the industry and willingness to pay. Please see key takeaways and detailed notes from the call below.Key Takes

·         Overall Views

- Yes, the problem is significant - it takes 1–2 days just to make a 30–60 sec ad editor-ready (from camera footage transfer to First Cut). Agencies are willing to pay around ₹20–25k/month (comparable to an assistant editor’s salary, slightly lower due to human QC still being needed). Large production houses can pay more since the time burden increases with longer content, while freelancers and small studios typically cap at ₹10–15k/month given limited budgets and simpler file renaming/restructuring needs.

·         Pain points

- Transfer and backups – For a 30–60s ad, transferring footage usually takes 20 minutes to 2 hours depending on shots and hardware. At agency scale with 500GB–1TB footage, it can stretch to 1–3 hours since teams copy twice and check before wiping cards.

- Proxy creation and organization – Biggest bottleneck. For a 30–60s ad it takes about half a day, while multi-cam or long-form can take 1–1.5 days. Editors must create lighter proxies(small, edit-friendly files) from heavy raw footage to make editing possible. Crashes are common due to heavy software or weak hardware, so the process is often repeated. Directors also mark “good” or “best” takes on paper during shoot, and assistant editors later have to hunt for those clips and tag them manually while creating bins.

- First rough cut – While setting up the timeline, junior editors spend hours finding the “good” and “best” takes marked by the director on paper during the shoot and placing them in the draft. They also manually scan for the right b-roll shots like “walking” or “coffee” which slows the process further.

- Cloud sharing and relinking – Smaller agencies prefer sending physical drives because cloud is expensive. Larger houses use cloud but complain that Dropbox and AWS are smooth but costly, while Google Drive is cheap but slow and splits files into 4GB chunks, making 500GB–1TB uploads painful.

·         Willingness to pay

- Agencies are open to ₹20–25k per month (about the cost of an assistant editor) if the tool saves at least one full day of setup per project.

- Freelancers are more cost sensitive and only willing to pay ₹10–15k per month if the tool clearly saves hours and lets them take on more jobs.

·         What matters for adoption

- Must save 8–12 hours between “camera card out” and “project ready to edit” for a 30–60s ad.

- Accuracy is critical, especially in correctly tagging the “good” and “best” takes and ensuring safe backups.

- Needs to integrate as a plugin inside Adobe or Final Cut, since editors avoid standalone apps that slow systems.

- Affordable and fast cloud option that can handle 500GB–1TB uploads and downloads in one go.

·         Pushbacks and risks

- Editors fear new software will slow their machines, so a plugin-like approach is preferred.

- Assistant editors are cheap in India (₹25–30k per month), so ROI must be very visible.

- Larger production houses are slow to adopt since senior editors rely on assistants and resist new tools.

### Detailed Notes from Call Dheeman Garg (Freelance Photographer; also shoots Z47 podcasts)

·         The problem today

·         DIT and transfer

- After the shoot ends, cards come in from each camera.

- Files are copied to a main SSD, then a second backup is made. Many teams also start a cloud backup.

- Copies must be checked before cards are wiped and returned to the rental house.

- For 30sec this can take anywhere between 20misn – 1hrs but speed highly depends on hardware and no of shots.

- A slow cable or cheap card can turn minutes into hours.

- People worry most about losing footage, so extra time goes into double-checking copies.

·         Organization and bins, and creating proxies

- Proxies (small, edit-friendly files) can be created in-camera but depend on camera model. If not, they are made on the computer so editing is smoother.

- Creating proxy is not a big issue can be done using Adobe etc.

- After proxies, bins are created based on camera, scene, shot type, and speaker.

- Clips are dropped into these folders. Full file-by-file renaming is not usual in small scale setup like by shot or scene unless the shoot was super messy.

- And when the cameraman and Asst. editor are the same person I know while shooting which are the good shots – so can organize quickly in like 2-3 hrs for 30sec Ad.

- Also, if the director marked good or best takes on paper, those clips also needs to be tagged or added in Adobe timeline while making rough cut so the editor finds them fast.

·         A/V sync and a quick first cut

- Matching sound to video is mostly handled well by built-in tools in Final Cut and Premiere, or simple add-ons.

- Only for podcasts and talk shows, a small hardware switcher can cut live, so a rough cut is ready at the end.

·         Cloud sharing and review

- Still an issue - Big folders needs to be uploaded for the editor and client.

- Google Drive is common but slow for large files and often downloads in batch of 4GB zips folders which is not ideal.

- Dropbox is sometimes smoother but less common with clients.

- Frame.io is used for review once files are online; the slow part is getting big files uploaded.

·         What the new tool can add

·         DIT and transfer

- One button to copy to two or more drives at once, with automatic checks and a simple report that shows every file copied safely.

- Clear speed meter and warnings like “this cable or reader is slow.”

- Block card formatting until safe copies are confirmed.

·         Organization and bins, and creating proxies

- If proxies are missing, auto-create them during copy and link them to the originals.

- Apply a clean folder template per client or editor (by camera, scene, shot type, speaker).

- Quick tags for “good” and “best take” if the director marked them.

·         Cloud sharing and review

- More affordable cloud solution which lets user upload huge (500GB/1TB) files in one go without break and download also as a single file instead in batches like google drive.

- Easy handoff to Frame.io when upload finishes.

·         Person’s willingness to pay

- Agencies are open to a subscription if it removes junior setup hours or avoids an extra hire, and if it never risks data. Junior editors earn about ₹25–30k per month

- Freelancers will consider it if it clearly saves hours and lets them take more jobs each week; though many are tired of too many subscriptions so need to be accurate.

·         Potentials ICP’s

- Agencies and production teams that do many shoots with multiple cameras (podcasts, events, ad films), have more data to transfer, and more organization of files.

- Less likely at the start: solo freelancers since they shoot smaller video and don’t organize much since they do it end to end so don’t need much organization

·         Pushbacks and risks

- Trust and accuracy: people want near-perfect safety. If a human still has to recheck everything, the value drops.

- Cost Concern: assistant labor is cheap and experienced teams are already fast, so savings may look small to some buyers.

- Hardware limits: slow cards, readers, and cables will still slow things. Software can warn you but cannot fix bad hardware.

- New tool fatigue: editors may resist another standalone app. A plugin-like feel or a very simple one-screen app will help adoption.

### Detailed Notes from Call with Zohrab Reys (Runs agency 1g4T; Has worked on Ads with cultfit, Acko etc)

·         The problem today

·         Camera transfer and backups

- After a shoot, all the camera footage is copied from memory cards to local drives and needs to be uploaded to cloud or create multiple back-ups.

- This can take anywhere from 30 minutes to 3 hours depending on how much was shot/, and the hardware used.

- People see this as big point but huge cost wise – because usually done by DIT in India which cost ~20k per month

·         Make smaller edit-friendly files (proxies)

- The raw camera files are very heavy and slow to edit. So editors create smaller copies (proxies) that are less resolution and lighter to work with.

- This is the single biggest pain point according to the agency person – “half a day to a day goes in just creating proxies.”

- Current software often crashes in the middle, so someone has to keep watching and break the work into smaller batches.

·         Organize and name files

- Editors want files arranged in their own style — for example, some prefer by camera, some by scene, some by shot or take, some by actor.

- Clips also need to be tagged by camera, by speed (normal vs slow motion), and by orientation (vertical vs horizontal).

- For Audio recording; Editor needs separate folders for sound, music, and effects.

- Also, during the shoot, the director makes notes of the good and best takes on paper with the clip number or some detail of the clip. Later, editors must manually find those clips and tag or rename them as “good take” or “best take” in the system.

- All this organizing and renaming is slow and repetitive.

·         Match audio and video

- Sound and video have to be synced together.

- Today, tools like Premiere do this automatically for most clips — about 90% accuracy.

- Dubbing shots cannot be automatically synced by Adobe but getting dubbing done and syncing it up is quite cheap and all can be done in studio incl. audio recording in a day. So, this is not a major issue anymore.

·         First rough cut

- By this stage, files are copied, smaller versions made, folders created, and sound matched.

- A junior editor then makes the first draft. This is not creative work - they just line up the selected takes on a timeline for, say, a 30-second ad. The creative touches are added later by the senior editor.

- Sometimes, to do this, the junior editor goes back to the director’s paper notes, finds the clip numbers marked as good or best, and manually places them on the timeline if not tagged properly while creating bins and renaming files.

- For extra b-roll shots (like walking, coffee, or office shots), they have to manually scan through many clips to find the right ones. This is slow. So, if with AI prompt in folder - can search for all the walking or coffee shots, it becomes way easier (again this is if renaming is not done in v detail which is sometimes not done in small scale projects)

·         Sharing with other editors

- Video files are huge. Often teams still courier hard drives or use tools like MyAirBridge or Sendspace.

- Google Drive splits large files into 4GB chunks, which makes downloading painful. As the person said, “you will take an entire day checking.”

- Lighter proxy files can be shared online, but later connecting them back to the full-quality video takes extra steps.

·         Client review

- Clients give time-stamped feedback through Frame.io, which works well.

- This is not seen as a pain point.

·         What they want from a new tool

- Proxies should be created automatically, without crashing, and grouped correctly by camera, speed, orientation, shots, scenes, actors.

- Folders should be auto-created (by camera, sound, effects, music).

- The tool should read the director’s shoot notes (photo or doc) and automatically tag clips as “good take” or “best take.”

- The tool should help junior editors find the right clips quickly — for example, if they type “walking,” it should show all walking clips from that shoot.

- The final output should be a ready-to-open project in Premiere, Final Cut, or DaVinci.

- It should ideally be a plugin that works inside existing tools, not a brand-new software because new software always slows down the system

·         Willingness to pay

- Must save at least a day of work or cost less than hiring an assistant editor (~₹25-30k/month).

- He has seen quotes of ₹8k–₹12k per reel for AI animation, but freelancers do the same for that price and are preferred because they can handle changes quickly. In AI tools, have to give a lot of context.

·         Who will use first

- Ad agencies and production houses (ads and branded content).

- Long-form teams (web series, reality shows) where organizing and sequencing takes lots of time.

- Solo creators don’t really need it or afford

·         Success measure

- Save 8-12 hours between “camera card out” and “project ready to edit” for 30-60s Ad.

·         Pushbacks and risks

- People prefer tools they already know, even if new tools are better.

- Accuracy is a concern - if the tool mislabels or misses clips, editors won’t trust it.

- Training and adoption is tough, especially in bigger production houses that are old-fashioned and resistant to change.
