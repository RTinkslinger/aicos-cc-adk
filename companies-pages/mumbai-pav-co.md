### Company Background & Founding Story

- One founder is a commerce graduate turned chef with culinary training in France and two years at a Michelin star restaurant in Sweden, plus experience at MASK (India's #1 rated restaurant for 5 years)  

- Both founders met at MESA incubator, bringing complementary skills: one from operational/kitchen background, the other from front-end/marketing background (Tuk Tuk Thai and Shyamji's experience)  

- Conducted customer research with ~200 customers to understand dining habits, consumption patterns, wallet share per meal, and identify underserved categories  

### Market Opportunity & Differentiation

- Identified Mumbai street food as an underserved category - customers couldn't name 5 brands in 5 seconds (unlike burgers, pizza, coffee, desserts) 

- Existing players were mostly unorganized standalone shops with no delivery focus 

- Only Goalie (Vada Pav) had tried to organize this category but is no longer operational 

- Category estimated at 350+ outlets across India, suggesting large opportunity for organized play 

### Current Operations & Traction

- **Store count**: 6 operational stores, 3 in pipeline, targeting 10 by March 2025 

- **Launch timeline**: Started August 2024 with first store (250 sq ft bootstrapped), scaled to Bangalore  

- **Store formats being tested**: High streets, kiosks (225 sq ft at Ritz-Carlton), cloud kitchens, tech parks, malls  

- **Revenue**: Annualized revenue of ₹8 crores from 6 stores, targeting ₹10 crores by March and ₹14 crores by April 

- **AOV**: ₹220+ on dine-in, ₹350+ on delivery 

- **Store profitability**: Achieved within 45 days of launch 

- **Repeat rates**: 40% monthly repeat rate (customers reordering within 30 days), versus 12-15% benchmark for Bangalore on Zomato  

### Menu Strategy

- Five broad categories identified from customer research: 

  - Vada Pav, Bhajiya Pav, Samosa Pav (hawker favorites)
  - Pav Bhaji and Tawa Pulao (night/late night consumption)
  - Mumbai Grilled Sandwich (student and corporate favorite)
  - Tea time snacks (Bun Maska, mango shake, sitafal shake)

- Wider category range allows attraction of customers across different meal times, unlike competitors with limited time slots 

- Building "India's fast food" brand, not just Vada Pav - positioning as Mumbai street food company  

### Operational Model

- **Central kitchen approach**: Supplies raw materials, base ingredients, and flavors; final assembly done at outlets 

- **Prep division**: Longer shelf life items (spice mixes) made centrally; shorter shelf life items (<24 hours, like cooked potatoes) done at outlets to reduce dependency and maintain freshness  

- **Vendorization plans**: Aim to vendorize items like bhaji in next 3 months to reduce central kitchen dependency 

- **10-minute delivery**: Positioned for quick delivery in 3km radius through quick commerce, leveraging street food's assembly-friendly nature 

### Store Staffing & Training

- **Staff per store**: 10 people across two shifts (5-6 per shift) 

- **Staff breakdown**: 2 people outside (order taking, customer area), 4 in kitchen (3 cooking across frying/tawa/grilling sections, 1 dispatching)  

- **Training time**: 3 days for staff to learn all required skills 

- **Attrition**: 15% monthly attrition rate 

### Quality Control & Consistency

- **Four-pronged approach**:    

  - Customer reviews tracking
  - Random ordering across outlets for testing
  - SOP tracking and shelf life reporting
  - Digital checklist requiring managers to taste all items 3 times daily

- **Future plans**: Introducing automated fryers to standardize cooking levels

- **California Burrito**: Strong store-level economics, category creation, 35-40% repeat rates, profitable outlets with 1.5-year payback   

- **McDonald's**: Operational benchmarks including 90-second order-to-delivery time, consistency across locations, robust L&D program with 5 universities, handbook addressing all problems to combat attrition  

- **Samosa Party**: Executing well in Indian food QSR space 

- **Haldiram**: ₹1000+ crore restaurant business, front-end appears as family dining but back-end operates like QSR 

### Challenges in QSR Category

- **Low entry barriers**: Easy for many players to start with plug-and-play vendor models, leading to high failure rates 

- **Part-time mentality**: Many operators treat it as side hustle rather than full-time focus 

- **Success factors**: Remaining focused on single category and single region to build economies of scale, procurement leverage, deep expertise, and reduce competition vulnerability 

### Strategic Considerations Discussed

- **AOV expansion**: 15% increase achieved through introducing drinks category (₹170-165), plans to add desserts and combo offerings  

- **Aggregator strategy**: Only listing one outlet on Dineout/Zomato dine-in to avoid offering discounts on dining; better representation on Google 

- **Food court learnings**: Best sellers perform well but add-ons (beverages) underperform due to specialized competitors nearby; group dining leads to multi-brand ordering rather than single-brand  

- **Expansion considerations**: Operational capabilities stronger in South India; considering Hyderabad, Chennai while being measured with growth to maintain quality and supply chain efficiency  

### Investor Feedback & Decision

- **Z47's assessment**:    

  - Very high bar for QSR investments
  - Founders "not bad but nothing exceptional"
  - Category not sufficiently differentiated (every market has street food)
  - Prefer non-linear business models over linear store-by-store expansion
  - 10% EBITDA at ₹20 lakh stores "not great" - economics typically degrade as businesses scale
  - First few outlets often have better economics that aren't replicable at scale
  - Network-wide EBITDA likely to settle at 8-10%

- **Key investor concerns**:    

  - Things get difficult at 10-20 outlet scale due to competition, food consistency issues, or location choices
  - 15% monthly attrition means 150 people leaving monthly at 100-store scale
  - Important metrics to track: customer feedback, NPS, repeat rates, same-store sales growth
