**3. Machinery**

*At every stage of the crop cycle, for a specific operation on the farm, the farmer has a choice - whether to use manual labour, animal labour or farm machinery. The tipping point for mechanisation of a particular operation in a particular crop in a specific geography is when the following equation tips in favour of net cost of machinery. Then, we can safely say that the machine has product-market-fit.*

**Machine Hyperlocal Product Market Fit**

| Operating Cost (Fuel + Operator + Capex Amortised) + Input Cost + Revenue Loss/Gain = Total Cost per Acre for Operation + Intangible Benefits (Time Saved, Drought Reduced, Weather dependence reduced, etc) | VS | Labour Cost + Input Cost + Revenue Loss/Gain = Total Cost per Acre for Operation + Intangible Benefits (Flexibility, etc) |

- **This PMF is very hyperlocal in nature.** In every geography, the labour cost and availability varies and the exact SKU of the machine also varies due to crop and soil type thus changing this equation. We do believe however that the equation will be very similar for a big farm and a small farm in the same geography and the mechanisation solutions will converge too.

### Examples:

- **Soybean Sowing is entirely mechanised** in most locations with a tractor pulled seed cum fertiliser drill. Time is of essence as these are rainfed crops and need to be sown when soil conditions are perfect. Every day of delay reduces yield by a quintal. Plant density is high so human/bullocks take too long. There is some cost increase due to seed drill dropping too many seeds and revenue loss due to lower germination rate given the irregular depth of sowing. However, net net, the equation tilts in favour of machinery.

- **Cotton Sowing in Rahuri is still performed by hand** as cotton is a sensitive seed with low plant density and low depth of sowing for better germination. As maize and soybean sowing are mechanised, labour is available for cotton sowing thus tilting the equation in favour of labour.
