**Latin American Market Evolution**

- 10 years ago, Latin America was as fragmented as India; shifted entirely

- Shift driven by OEMs (manufacturers)

- 300,000 fragmented building material stores in Latin America

- Organized retail now accounts for 5-10% of the market

- Two major building materials players (Holcim, CEMEX) opened distribution businesses

- Converted existing retail touchpoints to organized format; very successful

- Distribution business set up separately from manufacturing business

**Holcim’s Disensa Network**

- 3,000 stores (adding 300-400 stores per year)

- Targets primarily contractors

- Built with limited SKUs; not full-scale tile retail

- Traditional cement channel upgraded

- Not large format retail; typical store size 500-1,000 square feet

- Offers basic tiles, roofing tiles, and some sanitary ware

**Thailand Market Parallel**

- SCG Group (cement player) entered retail distribution space

- Offers products beyond cement: tiles, sanitary ware, faucets, surfaces

**Market Evolution Opinion**

- Big box format (e.g., DIY Home Depot) won’t work in India

- Model with everything from cement to tiles in same shop might not work

- Potential for stores with overlapping consumer journeys

- US market differs: more installer-led, specification-first approach

- US doesn’t need the same kind of consumer experience; big box format works there

**Current Indian Market Structure**

- Tile dealers typically stock tiles + some sanitary ware

- Small sub-dealers for tiles in smaller cities (especially in West Bengal, UP)

- 70-80% of tile dealers primarily sell tiles, with sanitary ware as secondary

- Organized players account for 20-25% of the market

**Consumer Journey and Brand Relevance**

- 25% of consumers (mass to premium segment) care about brand in tiles

- 30% are design-centric, brand doesn’t matter

- 15% are influencer-driven (architect, interior designer, contractor)

- 20% are value-focused

**Inventory and Distribution Model**

- Industry standard: build-to-ship-to model

- Hippo Stores claims to use this model, but has significant working capital per store

- Sanitary ware typically requires inventory due to volume-to-weight ratio and damage risks

**Challenges in Category Aggregation**

- Different purchase occasions for various categories (tiles, sanitary ware, kitchen)

- Aggregation has limited value due to distinct consumer journeys

- No clear network effect or economy of scale for multi-category stores

**Potential Business Models**

- Focus on tiles as a wedge category (direct dealership model, better margins)

- Consider online customer acquisition with offline fulfillment

- Explore no-inventory models for certain categories

- Potential for VR/AR to enhance visualization and reduce store visits
